## Best Practices

### Creating Effective Decision Trees

‚úÖ **Start simple** - Complex trees are hard to follow  
‚úÖ **Use binary splits when possible** - Yes/No is clearest  
‚úÖ **Prioritize by impact** - Most important factors first  
‚úÖ **Document exceptions** - Note edge cases  
‚úÖ **Test with examples** - Walk through real scenarios  
‚úÖ **Update regularly** - Frameworks drift without maintenance  

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

‚ùå Too many branches (analysis paralysis)  
‚ùå Vague criteria (hard to apply consistently)  
‚ùå No escalation path (what if nothing fits?)  
‚ùå No documentation of actual decisions (can't improve)  
‚ùå Static framework (never updated)

## When to Use This Template

**Use for:**
- Recurring strategic decisions
- Complex multi-factor choices
- Process standardization
- Training new team members on decision logic
- Regulatory or compliance-driven decisions
- Resource allocation decisions

**Don't use for:**
- One-off, unique decisions (overkill)
- Simple binary choices (too much overhead)
- Purely emotional or subjective decisions
- Decisions requiring expert judgment case-by-case

## Decision Matrix Alternative

**For decisions with multiple weighted criteria:**

| Option | Criterion 1 (Weight: X) | Criterion 2 (Weight: Y) | Criterion 3 (Weight: Z) | Total Score |
|--------|-------------------------|-------------------------|-------------------------|-------------|
| Option A | Score | Score | Score | Weighted Total |
| Option B | Score | Score | Score | Weighted Total |
| Option C | Score | Score | Score | Weighted Total |

**Use a decision matrix when:**
- Multiple viable options exist
- Criteria can be weighted and scored
- Objective comparison is valuable

**Use a decision tree when:**
- Logic flows through sequential questions
- Each branch eliminates certain paths
- Clear IF/THEN structure exists
